# Operator Debrief Script

This script is for your reference, not the participant's. Used during the
3-minute wind-down between the controller's debrief screen and saying
goodbye. The controller has already captured the structured debrief
responses; this is the informal conversation.

## CRITICAL: do not disclose the affect-detection framing

Anywhere in the conversation, do NOT mention any of:

- "Affect", "emotion", "frustration", "engagement", "flow"
- That we're trying to detect mental states from typing or mouse data
- That the campaign decision had a "gotcha" in the Excel data
- That we're combining behavioral and physiological signals
- Any reference to UEBA, behavioral profiling, or the underlying methodology

The full cohort needs to be naive to this framing. One participant
mentioning it on LinkedIn or in a Slack channel can contaminate the rest
of recruitment.

If asked directly "what is the study really about?", deflect with:

> "The full briefing will come after we close recruitment. Right now we're
> asking everyone the same things to keep the data clean. I can send you
> the full write-up once data collection ends in [month]."

This is the truth, not a lie. The full briefing will indeed come at
study close.


## Things to actually say (the wind-down)

Pick from these as the moment allows. No need to hit all of them.

**Acknowledgement.**
> "Thanks for the time, that's the formal part done."

**Equipment removal.**
> "You can take off the chest strap now. The session controller will
> have finished saving everything by the time you're standing up."

**Casual checks on their experience.**
> "How was the brief? Did the scenario make sense?"
> "Did the timing feel reasonable?"
> "Anything technical that didn't work for you?"

If they bring up specific issues (Excel formula they couldn't get to work,
file they couldn't find, frustration with the email client) — listen,
acknowledge, write a one-line note in your session journal. Don't try to
solve it now.

**On their recommendation.**

It's natural for participants to want to know if they got the "right"
answer. Resist giving them feedback on substance. Use:

> "There's no single right answer — that's why we ran the case the way
> we did. Different people approach the same data with different
> instincts and we want to see the range."

This is true and also avoids tipping off that there was a gotcha
specifically about Enterprise economics.

**Closing.**
> "If anything comes to mind in the next few days that you'd want us to
> know, just email me. Otherwise, you'll hear from me when the study
> closes."

Then walk them out.


## What to write in your session journal afterward

A quick note immediately after they leave, while it's fresh:

- Anything unusual about the equipment (Polar disconnect, OBS error,
  strange timing, etc.)
- Anything the participant said that you'd want as context when
  analysing their data
- Your impression of their engagement level (energetic? distracted?
  visibly tired?) — this won't be in the data otherwise
- Anything about the protocol that needs to change for future sessions

Keep these in a single text file or markdown doc per participant. They
become part of the methodological audit trail.


## If a participant figured out the affect-detection framing

It happens. Some participants are sharp, or have background in HCI or
psych research, or simply guess from the equipment.

If they say so explicitly during the debrief:

> "Smart guess. I can't confirm or deny right now because we need to
> keep the rest of the cohort naive — but I'll send you the full
> write-up at study close so you can see if you were right."

Then make a note in your journal: "participant suspected the affect
angle". This is important data for the methodology section — you'll
report what fraction of participants figured out the design.

Do not exclude their data on this basis alone. The methodology will
report it as a covariate, not a disqualification.
